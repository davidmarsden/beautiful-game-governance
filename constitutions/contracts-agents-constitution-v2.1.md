The Beautiful Game

Contracts, Agents, Ambition & Club Attractiveness Constitution v2.1

---

Design Philosophy

Football clubs do not buy players. Football clubs persuade players.

A transfer is not the acquisition of an asset. It is an agreement between human beings with different goals, incentives and ambitions.

Money matters. Success matters. Opportunity matters. Relationships matter. Different players value these things differently.

The purpose of this Constitution is to ensure that player movement is governed by persuasion rather than acquisition.

---

Constitutional Principles

Players Are Participants, Not Property — players make decisions, clubs negotiate, managers persuade, agents advise. No club is entitled to sign a player merely because it offers the highest fee.

Players Have Agency — players are independent actors with careers, ambitions and preferences of their own. Clubs, managers and agents may influence decisions; none controls the player.

Preferences Are Read, Not Known (new) — what a player values is public in outline but never in exact measure. Managers learn a player's true preferences the same way they learn his ceiling: by scouting and regional knowledge, narrowing an estimate they can never make perfect. Understanding people is a scouted skill, not free information.

Every Transfer Requires Three Agreements — the selling club, the buying club and the player must all agree. Failure of any one prevents the transfer.

Ambition Is Personal — there is no universally "best" club. The same offer may attract one player and repel another.

Reputation Is Earned — club attractiveness and manager reputation emerge from performance, derive from published rules and public information, and are never assigned manually.

Promises Have Consequences — keeping promises builds trust; breaking them damages reputation.

---

Shared Definition — Tenure

«Tenure = the number of consecutive seasons a manager has spent at his current club.»

Three systems use it differently: Building Credit (Youth Discovery Constitution) accrues through successful seasons and resets when tenure ends; Manager Reputation (this Constitution) is career-cumulative and persists across clubs; Stability (below) is derived from current tenure only. The season count is shared; the resulting values are not.

1. Player Contracts

Every player must have an active contract or exist as a free agent. Contracts define Wage, Contract Length, Expiry Date, Signing Bonus (optional), Release Clause (optional), Loyalty Bonus (optional), Sell-On Clauses (where applicable). Contract terms are public; negotiations are private until completed.

Contract Expiry — the player becomes a free agent, the previous club loses ownership rights, and the player may negotiate with any club. No transfer fee is payable. Development Levy obligations remain in force for qualifying under-24 players.

Development Levy on Free Transfers — if an under-24 player joins after contract expiry, no fee is paid; the ACQUIRING club pays the Development Levy directly to qualifying Developing Owners per the Youth Discovery & Recruitment Constitution. The previous club receives nothing. This mirrors real-world training compensation while preserving youth-development incentives.

---

2. Agents

Every player is represented by an agent. Agents represent player interests; they are not neutral.

Agents influence wage demands, contract length, release clauses, signing bonuses, negotiation flexibility and preferred destinations.

Agent Behaviour — agents vary in style (Financial Maximiser, Career Builder, Loyalty Focused, Development Focused, Prestige Focused). Agent archetypes and their negotiation preferences are FULLY public. Unlike player preferences (§3), agent traits are not banded — the agent is a known quantity the manager negotiates through; the player is the one who must be read.

Agent Influence — agents influence decisions; they never override player preferences. Agent recommendations are one input into evaluation, never the sole determinant.

---

3. Player Preferences

Every player possesses a preference profile describing what he values when evaluating opportunities: Ambition, Loyalty, Wage Priority, Playing Time Priority, Trophy Priority, Development Priority, Home Region Preference, Pressure Tolerance, Stability Preference.

Preference Bands (revised — scouted, not published exactly)

A player's exact preference values are fixed but hidden. What is public is a BAND for each preference — a category and its range (for example, Wage Priority: High (60–80)). Every manager sees the same public band.

A manager's PERSONAL estimate of a preference narrows within that band through Knowledge Regions and scouting, using the same Clarity Factor that governs Potential (Youth Discovery & Recruitment Constitution §5):

Personal Preference Width = Public Band Width × ClarityFactor   (ClarityFactor ≥ 0.40)

Better scouting and stronger regional knowledge tighten the estimate toward the true value but can never reach it. The residual uncertainty is permanent and deliberate: it is what keeps recruitment a contest of judgement rather than a solved calculation. This mirrors exactly how Potential works — public band, private narrowing, no certainty — and makes "understanding people" a learnable, scouted skill rather than free information.

Preference Stability — core traits change slowly. Players may evolve through age and experience, but dramatic unexplained changes are prohibited. Behaviour must remain understandable and believable.

---

4. Club Attractiveness

Not a single score, but independently calculated factors; different players weight them differently.

Sporting Prestige — division level, league position, recent finishes, trophy history, continental qualification and performance.

Financial Strength — revenue, wage budget, financial stability, transfer-spending capacity.

Development Reputation — youth progression, successful sales, players reaching higher levels, development promises fulfilled. Particularly important to young players.

Stability — current manager tenure, ownership stability, financial stability, competitive consistency. Reflects the probability that today's promises remain credible tomorrow.

Playing Opportunity — squad depth, positional competition, tactical fit, expected playing time. Players can assess their likely role before signing.

The formulas for all five factors are in the Transfer Mechanics Appendix.

---

5. Manager Reputation

A public, career-cumulative profile, separate from Club Reputation, persisting across clubs. Components (all derived from published rules): promotions won, trophies won, average league performance, youth development record, promise fulfilment rate, contract commitment record, tenure stability.

Manager Archetypes — Youth Developer, Promotion Specialist, Elite Winner, Financial Builder, Long-Term Architect — emerge from conduct, never selected manually.

---

6. Promises

Managers may make promises during negotiations (First-Team Football, Development Pathway, Promotion Challenge, Trophy Ambition, Contract Review, Positional Role). Promises are recorded publicly.

Promise Evaluation — assessed automatically against published criteria (Transfer Mechanics Appendix). Success improves Manager Reputation; failure damages it. No administrator judgement.

Exceptional Circumstances — force-majeure rules (injury, suspension, severe loss of form) are defined in the Appendix. Managers are not penalised for circumstances outside their reasonable control.

---

7. Transfer Evaluation

Players evaluate all available offers. Evaluation is deterministic and the formula is in the Transfer Mechanics Appendix.

The game computes a player's choice from his TRUE (hidden) preferences, so the outcome is fully deterministic and reproducible. A manager, however, plans his offer from his SCOUTED ESTIMATE of those preferences (§3), and therefore cannot compute the exact winning bid — only an estimated range. This is the intended uncertainty: competitive and informational, never random.

Factors include wage package, contract security, playing opportunity, club attractiveness, manager reputation, agent preferences and personal preferences. No single factor guarantees success.

Transfer Acceptance — the highest financial offer may lose; the most prestigious club may lose; the nearest club may lose. Players choose the offer that best matches their preferences. This is intentional.

---

8. Free Agents

Free agents negotiate directly with interested clubs. The absence of a fee raises the importance of wages, signing bonuses, playing opportunity and club attractiveness. A qualifying Development Levy remains payable (§1).

---

9. Youth Recruitment

Young players generally weight Development Reputation, Playing Opportunity, Manager Reputation and Long-Term Pathway more heavily; financial considerations less so. This creates genuine advantages for well-run development clubs.

---

10. Veteran Recruitment

Older players may weight wages, trophy opportunities, contract security and geographic preference more heavily. Priorities evolve through a career.

Relationship to Other Constitutions

The Player Rating Constitution defines Ability, Potential, Form and Reputation.

The Youth Discovery & Recruitment Constitution governs scouting, discovery, recruitment windows, player identification, the Development Levy, and the Clarity Factor — which this Constitution now also uses to narrow preference estimates.

The World Constitution governs ownership, squad limits, finances, transfer regulations, contract administration, and the dismissal and reappointment systems.

This Constitution governs how players, agents, managers and clubs interact once recruitment interest exists. The deterministic formulas are in the Transfer Mechanics Appendix.

---

Golden Rule

Clubs compete for players. Players choose clubs. Agents influence negotiations. Money influences decisions. None of these alone determines the outcome.

The Beautiful Game rewards persuasion, trust, reputation and opportunity as much as financial power. A successful manager must understand people as well as football — and, because preferences are scouted not given, understanding people is now a genuine skill, not a lookup.
