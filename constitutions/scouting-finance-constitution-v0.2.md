The Beautiful Game

Scouting & Finance Constitution v0.2

The economic framework: it decides whether "football knowledge beats financial power" survives contact with human managers.

---

Design Philosophy

Money is real, and the game must model it. But money buys reach, infrastructure and attractiveness — never certainty, and never the edge that genuine football knowledge confers.

Not every manager watches lots of football. They must still be able to commit, compete and win — through other strengths. Success in any domain earns reward; managers direct those rewards where they choose. Over the long term no one should be hopelessly advantaged or disadvantaged by money alone.

---

Constitutional Principles

Money Matters But Does Not Dominate — finances shape what is possible, not who wins. A well-run club gains lasting advantages; a poorly-run one suffers. Neither is decisive on its own.

Reality For Players, Equality For Boards — the game mirrors real football for players, value and development, because that is what makes football knowledge the edge. It deliberately does NOT mirror ownership. Real boards are football's most arbitrary force — an owner's chequebook decides more than any manager's skill — and mirroring them would let money override merit. So every club's board is equally supportive, and backing is earned purely on merit relative to expectation. This is the one place the game overrides reality, precisely because reality there would wreck the meritocracy.

Earned Knowledge Outranks Bought Scouting — bought scouting can never read a player as well as genuine regional knowledge. Money buys breadth; knowledge buys depth; where they meet, depth wins. A constitutional invariant, not a balance setting.

Many Paths To Success — winning now, building for the future, financial stewardship and recruitment judgement are separate, separately-rewarded domains. A manager who never watches football can still thrive by excelling at another.

Bounded Divergence — the economy compresses rather than amplifies, through merit and solidarity, never through unequal boards. Diminishing returns, caps and solidarity keep small clubs viable and stop giants running away.

Determinism & Transparency — every figure, cost, expectation and reward derives from published formulas and public data. No administrator discretion. Manager choice is not discretion: managers may direct their own rewards.

1. Revenue

Five public streams, each computed deterministically.

Matchday = Stadium Capacity × Attendance% × Ticket Price. Attendance% rises with club reputation, league position and division.

Broadcasting = Central Pool distributed with a deliberate solidarity bias (§9): a large equal share by division plus a smaller merit share by league position. The equal:merit ratio is the primary compression dial.

Commercial / Sponsorship = a published curve on club reputation, Profile and division.

Prize Money = fixed published amounts per competition and finishing position.

Player Trading = transfer fees received, plus Development Levy receipts (Youth Discovery §13).

---

2. Wages and the ExpectedWage Curve

Wage Budget = a published fraction of projected revenue, bounded by a maximum wage-to-revenue ratio (the core FFP constraint, §6).

ExpectedWage = BaseWage(Ability) × ReputationModifier × AgeModifier

BaseWage(Ability) — published curve, interpolated logarithmically (annual, indicative units):

Ability| BaseWage
90| 10.0
80| 4.0
70| 1.2
60| 0.4
50| 0.12
40| 0.04

ReputationModifier = 1 + 0.005 × (Reputation − 50). AgeModifier: ≤21 → 0.85; 22–24 → 0.95; 25–30 → 1.00; 31–33 → 0.95; 34+ → 0.90.

Feeds the Wage offer dimension and the Contract Review good-faith minimum in the Transfer Mechanics Appendix.

---

3. Transfers and Spending

Transfer Budget = reserves + projected surplus + sales proceeds, within FFP limits.

Transfer-Fee Ceiling — no fee may exceed a published multiple of the player's current market value (prototype: 2.0×). The general anti-inflation rule, complementing the Development Levy's real-value basis.

Instalments, sell-on clauses and signing bonuses are permitted within budget and FFP rules.

---

5. The Scouting Economy

Scouting capability is bought through an annual scouting budget setting the club's Scouting Level, which defines the ScoutingFactor in the Clarity Factor (Youth Discovery §5, Appendix §2.2).

Scouting Level Costs (annual, share of revenue — indicative)

Level| ScoutingFactor| Annual Cost
None| 1.00| 0%
Basic| 0.90| ~1%
Good| 0.82| ~3%
Strong| 0.74| ~6%
Elite| 0.65| ~12%

Basic is affordable to any club. A small club CAN reach Elite, but only by spending a large share of its budget — a real trade-off against wages and transfers. Money buys breadth and reach across the world.

The Invariant (Earned Knowledge Outranks Bought Scouting)

Best bought clarity (Elite, 0.65) is constitutionally fixed strictly WORSE than best earned clarity (deepest Knowledge Region, 0.60). No spending narrows a band as far as genuine regional knowledge.

- A rich non-watcher with Elite scouting reads any player at 0.65 — competent everywhere.
- A poor specialist who truly follows a region reads its players at 0.60 — better, where he knows.
- Only earned depth plus bought breadth (0.60 × 0.65) approaches the 0.40 floor.

Money buys the ability to assess players everywhere; knowledge buys the best assessment somewhere. Where they meet, the specialist sees more clearly. The gap is a dial; the ordering is not.

---

6. Financial Fair Play and Sustainability

Clubs operate within published limits: Wage-to-Revenue Ratio ≤ a published maximum; cumulative losses ≤ a published threshold over a rolling window.

Breaches trigger deterministic, escalating sanctions: transfer restrictions, wage restrictions, then points deductions (World Constitution §7.3). No discretion — the trigger is the formula. Sustained surplus earns Board Backing (§8).

---

7. How Small Clubs Survive

Four published mechanisms, not charity: solidarity broadcasting (§1); solidarity/parachute payments for lower divisions and the recently relegated; Development Levy receipts (Youth Discovery §13); and promotion/prize money. Together they keep a well-run small club viable indefinitely without wealth.

---

8. Board Backing — the Discretionary Reward Economy

Every club's board is equally supportive (Reality For Players, Equality For Boards). Backing is not inherited wealth; it is earned by ONE rule applied identically to every manager: reward for achievement RELATIVE TO EXPECTATION.

Expectation (deterministic, public)

A season's expectation has two parts, neither admin-set:

- Expected Finish — derived by published formula from club reputation, division, squad strength and recent finishes: the level the club "should" reach.
- Contract Objective — the board's target agreed with the manager at signing (avoid relegation, top half, promotion, title challenge), public and fixed for the contract term. Defined with the contract machinery in the World Constitution.

Earning Backing — four domains, each measured AGAINST expectation:

- Silverware & Progress — trophies, promotions and finishes above Expected Finish / the Contract Objective.
- Future-Building — youth development beyond projection (Development Reputation OverGain), Building Credit, graduates.
- Financial Stewardship — operating surplus and revenue growth beyond what the club's means predict.
- Recruitment Judgement — signings whose value or Ability rose beyond expectation; low-Profile finds that became valuable.

Because the rule is expectation-relative, the same trophy pays a minnow far more than a giant: the minnow over-delivered, the giant met the brief. This is equal boards producing fair outcomes across unequal clubs — not a bias toward small clubs, but one meritocracy measured from each club's own starting line. It is the pattern the whole game already uses — reward beating expectation — applied to a manager's career.

Spending Backing — the manager directs his Backing, at his own choice, into published investments: scouting budget (raise Scouting Level), youth and development infrastructure, facilities and stability, or transfer/wage war chest. Manager choice, not administrator discretion. Two managers with identical Backing may invest completely differently — build on a strength, or fix a weakness.

The Cap — Backing spent on scouting can lift a non-scouter toward competence (Elite, 0.65) but never past the Earned-Knowledge Invariant (§5). Catch-up without overtaking. Backing-funded wage headroom is capped relative to organic revenue, so it supplements a club's economy rather than replacing it. Reward narrows gaps; it never buys certainty or the knowledge edge.

---

9. Bounded Divergence

The field compresses through merit and solidarity, never through unequal boards. Three mechanisms, applied equally to all:

- Solidarity revenue — broadcasting shared largely equally within a division (§1). The solidarity ratio is one compression dial.
- Expectation-relative Backing (§8) — giants who merely meet expectations earn little; over-performers (usually smaller, climbing clubs) earn most. Compression falls out of the merit rule itself, with no board treated more generously than another.
- Diminishing returns — accumulated Backing advantage is capped and its marginal value falls, so no one runs away. Its steepness is the second compression dial.

Plus the realistic climb: a well-run small club wins promotion, which grows its organic revenue (§1), closing the gap by rising rather than by handout.

Set mild: enough that a well-run, knowledgeable small club can climb over many seasons; never so much that wealth and success stop mattering.

Relationship to Other Constitutions

Defines the ExpectedWage curve, Scouting Levels and costs, revenue, wages, transfers, FFP and Board Backing — all referenced by the other documents.

The Player Rating Constitution supplies Ability, Reputation and the development projections used in wages and Backing. The Youth Discovery & Recruitment Constitution supplies the Clarity Factor (whose ScoutingFactor this document prices), Building Credit and the Development Levy. The Contracts Constitution and Transfer Mechanics Appendix consume the ExpectedWage curve and the transfer-fee ceiling. The World Constitution holds ownership, squad limits, the sanction machinery, and the contract / objective / dismissal machinery on which Expectation (§8) depends.

---

Golden Rule

Money buys breadth, reach and the means to compete. It never buys certainty, and it never buys the edge that genuine football knowledge confers.

Boards are equal; reward is earned, measured from each club's own expectations, and directed by the manager who earned it. Over a career, the richest club must not win simply by being rich — and the poorest, best-run, most knowledgeable manager must always be able to win by being right.
